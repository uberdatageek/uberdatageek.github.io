Begin Story

Half the US population lives in poverty or is low-income, according to US Census data. However, compared to most developed nations, Americans remain less concerned about rising Economic Inequality.

What divides the rich and poor?

Economic (Income) inequality is the unequal distribution of household or individual income across the various participants in an economy. There has been a significant increase in economic inequality in America since the 1970s. Roughly more than 70% of the world's countries have more equal income distribution than the US.

A widening disparity exist between wages and productivity, which signifies a shift in GDP share from labor to capital. Possible theories include...

Globalization

Low skilled American workers have been losing out to competition from low-wage workers in "Emerging" economies.

Skill-Biased

According to the skill-biased technical change theory income distribution favors high-skilled workers over low-skilled labor.

Super Star Hypothesis

Modern technologies of communication handsomely rewards winners while leaving losers empty handed.

Skill-Bias: Has high tech been truly beneficial to all?

“The notion of ‘skill-bias’ —and the literature that has recently blossomed around it—has introduced the theoretical possibility that technological progress benefits only a sub-group of workers, placing technical change also at the centerstage of the income distribution debate”
- Giovanni L. Violante

What is the Skill-Bias?

Skill biased technical change is defined as a shift in production technology that favors highly skilled or educated workers. Information technology leverages the abilities of a highly skilled worker to be exponentially more productive than an unskilled worker, therefore increasing their demand. View Skill-Bias Word Cloud

Case Study: Economic Inequality in California

Silicon Valley Tech Effect

The Impact of Technology (Skill-Bias) in California

The Gini coefficient (a measure of income inequality) for household income in the Bay Area was 48.7 in 2013. This is comparable to the Gini coefficient for the state as a whole (49.0) and greater than that of the entire United States (48.1) in 2013.

In viewing economic inequality through the looking glass of California, Skill-Biased technical change plays a major role. While many Americans agree that economic inequality is a big problem, most generally oppose the concept of wealth / income redistribution.

Why understand Skill-Bias

Understanding skill-bias is far reaching because policy makers could pursue initiatives that serve to lessen economic inequality. For instance, freely available online resources to build technical knowledge (e.g. Coursera, Big Data University, Udacity, edX, open.edu, Khan Academy et al) could be encouraged.

No Elixir, Just One First Step

There exist no silver bullet to solve the challenge of Economic Inequality. However, development of technical knowledge through education is a good step in the right direction.
